Capitalist Systems

Refresh my memory, what is THE key characteristic of a capitalist system?

  • (PRIVATE ownership of the means of production)


We assume:

  1. This creates a system in which individuals are motivated by the pursuit of profit, and

  2. This system functions most efficiently when markets are kept free

    • e.g. prices are set by supply and demand


Our work over the last few weeks has been an exploration of arguments connecting this system to ecological damage

  • We started with Donald Worster’s book on the Dust Bowl

  • What are the mechanisms in Worster’s argument? How specifically does capitalism cause ecological catastrophes like the Dust Bowl?

  • (SLIDE)

Worster’s Dust Bowl Argument

  • American culture is capitalist

  • Capitalism replaces our national identity with one focused on being an isolated, economically stressed individual (consumer)

  • Capitalism teaches us that nature is a resource and our survival is separate from nature’s survival

  • Capitalism shortens time horizons and creates incentives to ignore science

Therefore, capitalism caused the Dust Bowl

How much do you recognize these mechanisms in your personal behavior?

  • Which ones? Examples?


For this argument to be convincing, do we have to believe this is the dominant view of all people in our system? Why or why not?

Dauvergne’s Argument (Part 1)

Part 1 of Dauvergne’s book is meant to help us see how our human consumption in a capitalist system is, and has been, unsustainable


Based on the evidence and arguments in Part 1 of Dauvergne’s book, why is embedding environmentalism in a capitalist system doomed to create ecological problems?

  • (SLIDE)

Dauvergne’s Argument


The Exploitative Strategies of Capitalism

  1. Western imperialism

  2. Economic Globalization

  3. Environmentalism of the Rich

The capitalist system encourages individuals to seek profit

  • The promise of comfort through resource use


Pre-19th Century capitalism inspired the powerful to use imperialism as a tactic of maximizing profit

  • HUGELY destructive to the peoples and ecology of the developing world


After the world wars, imperialism got a bad name so a new tactic was needed and the world needed to be rebuilt

  • Welcome to the forces of globalization!

  • No longer about domination by an external state, now it’s about domination by the global markets and businesses

  • Per Dauvergne, this was also HUGELY destructive to the peoples and ecology of the developing world


Criticisms of globalization reach a crescendo so capitalism creates a new tactic: the environmentalism of the rich

  • Promotes cleaner processes and greater efficiency and the spread of corporate social responsibility

  • HOWEVER the efficiency just breeds more production and consumption which creates ecological doom and exploitation…

Dauvergne Evaluates Environmentalism (Chapter 7)

Big picture, in chapter 7 why does Dauvergne refer to environmentalism as a “movement of movements”?

  • Deep philosophical and policy differences have long characterized environmentalism.

  • Broad dimension running from radicals (remake the world economy!) to reformers (use institutions to guide globalization to do good).


According to Dauvergne, what are some of the broad accomplishments of the modern environmental movement?

  • e.g. How does he celebrate the movement he is about to savage with his critiques?


  • “National environmental organizations have also grown considerably since the 1970s” (79).

  • “Just about every country now has an environmental agency, and over a thousand International environmental treaties are in place” (79).

  • “Just about every culture has come to embrace the language of sustainability (80).

    • Swamps have become Wetlands, jungles have become biodiversity hotspots, whales are now seeing as sentient and majestic beings, not target practice like they were in the early part of the 20th century.

  • The environmental movement can even take credit for transforming ways of learning and educating citizens.

  • Meanwhile, billions of people are recycling and composting.


And yet, what is the HUGE caveat he ends chapter 7 with?

  • Despite all of this, we must acknowledge that at the global level of biodiversity, oceans, freshwater, rainforests, agricultural lands, and the climate are continuing to deteriorate.


SLIDE: How does a “movement of movements” produce bad outcomes?

Capitalism Creates an Environmentalism of the Rich


“More important, however, has been the innate power of consumer capitalism to distort and assimilate counternarratives and countermovements, especially critiques of wealth and economic growth” (p76).

Fundamentally, Dauvergne sees the capture of environmentalism as the next tactic of capitalism to continue exploiting the environment

  • Western imperialism gave way to globalization and globalization has adopted a “green” language in order to keep doing what it always has

Notes from the reading

Chapter 8

  • The life of Bruno Manser, a brave activist and the assault on activism
  • This guy really pushed the boundaries.
    • Does he go too far?
  • “The global campaign to save the world’s remaining rainforests has grown even bigger since Manser’s disappearance in 2000. Indigenous peoples and environmentalists continue to oppose loggers and organize public protests. Such resistance remains extremely dangerous for activists. Across 35 countries the NGO Global Witness found over 900 recorded murders of environmental activists from 2002 to 2013, with the average number of killings each year more than doubling after 2009” (96).
  • Many more have “disappeared” or suffered “accidents” with the Brazilian Amazon representing the most dangerous place for activists.
  • “…environmentalism of the rich has increasingly come to characterize rainforest activism as more and more environmentalists cooperate with multinational corporations and growth-oriented government’s, endorsing the value of international trade, foreign direct investment, and privatization, and relying on certification and eco-consumerism as solutions” (96-97).
  • Ending Deforestation?
    • “…but overall progress in slowing tropical deforestation has been frustratingly slow. Loopholes abound and implementation of regulations is generally weak” (97).
    • China “is now the largest overall consumer of tropical timber in Asia… Somewhere between 30 and 80% of the Timber flowing through China is illegal” (98).
    • Despite the work of the environmental movement and the creation of corporate alliances, the rate of tropical deforestation has been rising steadily since 2000.
    • “…governments and corporations around the world are now claiming that the end of deforestation is in sight, if only we can embrace state-led sustainable development, corporate-led responsibility, and consumer-led demand for sustainable products–three core messages of environmentalism of the rich” (100).

Chapter 9

  • My stab: Radical environmentalism is necessary but should move forward in non-violent ways.
  • The framing of eco-terrorism
    • Is Jane Goodall a radical or a sellout?
    • Patrick Moore, from Greenpeace to industry insider
    • Captain Paul Watson, becoming more militant over time with the Sea Shepard Conservation Society
  • Ecotage vs Eco-Terrorism
    • The Animal Liberation Front (ALF) and the Earth Liberation Front (ELF)
    • “…organize in secret cells and urge individuals to join by taking direct action to oppose industrial farming, animal testing, and animal cruelty. Such leaderless and underground structures can attract Lone Wolf zealots, and differentiating between legitimate and illegitimate acts is nearly impossible. Both movements, however, claimed to be nonviolent, urging followers to take precautions to try to avoid harming any life” (107).
  • Radical environmentalism is possible without violence
