Chapter notes: Prologue

NOTES

Wilderness is a subjective quality, not an object that can be identified

  • Everyone defines it differently and prioritizes different qualities when doing so PLUS the cultural definitions have also changed dramatically across time

Etymology of Wilderness

  • Early Teutonic and Norse languages the root is “will” meaning willful or uncontrollable
  • “Willed” becomes “wild” meaning lost, unruly, disordered or confused (in Old Swedish wild came from describing boiling water)
  • Old English adds animal (deor) and we get creatures not under the control of man (see the wildeor in Beowulf)
  • “wild-deor-ness” the place of wild beasts

Wilderness in Human Terms

  • Wilderness as a region where people get into “a disordered, confused or ‘wild’ condition (p2).
  • “bewilder” adds “be” and refers to a man in an alien environment where the civilization that normally structures our lives is absent

First uses of wilderness

  • Early 13th century, but more common in 14th when used in translations of the Bible, wilderness as treeless wasteland, “a desert; a tract of solitude and savageness” (Samuel Johnson 1755 Dictionary of the English Language)

Today

  • Dictionaries define wilderness as uncultivated or otherwise undeveloped land
  • Also include “any place in which a person feels stripped of guidance, lost, and perplexed

Interestingly, some German sources have paired this negative conception (wilderness as inhospitable, alien, mysterious) with one more positive (wilderness as beautiful, friendly, capable of elevating and delighting the beholder)

Wilderness as Classification

  • This becomes all the more challenging
  • How much human interference is allowable while still calling a place wild? How big does it have to be?
  • Various ballpark definitions across time: an area so large you cannot traverse it in one day (without mechanical means), or only if it can “absorb a two weeks’ pack trip” (p4-5)
  • US Forest Service experimented with terms like primitive, roadless and natural but they didn’t really solve the problem
  • The Outdoor Recreation Resources Review Commission (1962): Areas over 100,000 acres containing no roads usable by the public with “no significant ecological disturbance from on-site human activity”
  • A National Wilderness Preservation System (US Statutes at Large, 78 1964): an area where the earth and its community of life are untrammeled by man; must retain its primeval character and influence.

A Spectrum of Environments (p6-7)

  • Ranging from the “purely wild” to the “purely civilized”
  • The middle are the rural or pastoral environment (the ploughed) that represents a balance of the forces of nature and man
  • Moving from the middle to the wilderness pole means human influence appears less frequently; think man as an outpost in the wilderness
  • The opposite direction for increasing levels of human interference; think man shaping nature
  • Implies a shading or blending between two poles; Puts a premium on “variations of intensity rather than on absolutes”

Nash’s (2014) Argument


  1. The origination and use of “wilderness” as a label differed across cultures

  2. The meaning of wilderness has evolved over time

  3. No single classification of wilderness accepted broadly

Therefore, wilderness is an evolving, subjective “quality” NOT an object we can simply identify

    • Creatures not controlled by man (“wild-deor-ness”)

Different cultures and places came to the concept in different ways

  • For some wilderness is a state of disorder

  • Others focused on the places dominated by the beasts of the wild and their dangers

  • Still others proposed wilderness as a place where the constraint of society are removed and man becomes like a wild animal


These are all discrete and SEPARATE definitions of wilderness.

  • If you want to argue wilderness is a thing you have to grapple with the fact that different cultures looked at “it” and focused on very different elements of it

SLIDE: The basic requirements for an argument paper

Paper 1: The Importance of Wilderness


An argument paper MUST:

  1. Be organized around a single, clear thesis

  2. Include multiple premises

  3. Provide high quality evidence for all claims of fact

Your thesis is the conclusion of the paper

  • e.g. what you want the reader to accept


Your thesis must be supported by multiple premises

  • e.g. a premise is a reason for your conclusion


EACH premise must be supported by multiple examples of high quality evidence


SLIDE: The structure I am asking you to adopt

Five Paragraph Argument Paper

  1. Introduction Paragraph

  2. Body Paragraph: Reason 1 (premise)

  3. Body Paragraph: Reason 2 (premise)

  4. Body Paragraph: Reason 3 (premise)

  5. Conclusion Paragraph

This is the structure for a simple five paragraph essay.

  • This the old “tell them what you are going to tell them, tell them, then tell them what you told them” thing you’ve been doing since grade school


Your intro:

  • grabs the reader’s attention,

  • Convinces the audience they want to read your paper, and

  • Makes clear exactly what you want them to take away from reading it


Each body paragraph gives:

  • A reason supporting your conclusion,

  • Explains the reason in detail, and

  • Provides evidence to show this reason is consistent with reality!


Then the conclusion wraps it all up


More advanced papers simply innovate on this structure

  • Add more paragraphs to support and defend each premise

  • Add counter-argument paragraphs to raise and address the possibility that you could be wrong

  • etc
