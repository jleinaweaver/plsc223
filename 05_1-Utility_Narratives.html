A Concentrated Animal Feeding Operation (CAFO), also known as a factory farm

SLIDE: The problem

The CAFO Problem

CAFOS generate millions of tons of manure every year.

  • The most common type of facility to deal with this wase is an anaerobic lagoon e.g. a giant lake of poop


When properly managed these lagoons represent a substantial risk to the environment and public health

  • They threaten groundwater, local ecosystems and damage the air quality above them

Gifford Pinchot (1865-1946)

National Park Service Page

Forest History Society Page


Gifford Pinchot, born in 1865 and died in 1946, is one of the most important early voices in American environmentalism

  • Family fortune built, at least in part, on forest resources and so his lifelong interest in forests was fostered from a young age.

  • His dad sent him to France to study forestry because the US had no schools of forestry at the time


Long list of accomplishments

  • Helps start the Yale School of Forestry (money, money, money)

  • Founds the American Society of Foresters in 1900 “to increase awareness of forestry and provide professional development opportunities for those interested in making a career of protecting woodlands.”

  • Was appointed the first chief of the US Forest Service in 1905 by Teddy Roosevelt

  • Briefly led the National Conservation Commission (1910) performing studies on the resources of the US

  • Elected governor of Pennsylvania in 1922


Also, a long list of clashes with other environmental activists of the time

  • He and Muir butted heads A LOT


And why was that?

SLIDE: Let’s end by discussing the very cool work of Spirn

Spirn, A. (1996). “Constructing Nature: The Legacy of Frederick Law Olmstead.” Uncommon Ground.


Images: Niagara Falls, Boston Fens, Biltmore Estate


Ok, how does this Spirn article fit in our work today?

  • In other words, what is Spirn trying to show us in the work of Frederick Law Olmsted?


Explores the legacy of Olmstead’s masterful “parks” and the unforseen implications of creating the wilderness so expertly that people fail to realize they are constructed!

  • Olmstead presented as the halfway point on wilderness between Muir’s preservation and Pinchot’s workshop. An attempt to “reconcile reverence and use” (112)

  • Olmstead was very much working during the early conservation movement and trying hard to implement the ideas in practical and useful ways

  • Cool case studies: Yosemite, Niagara Falls, Biltmore, the fens in Boston

  • His work was double-edged. He was so good at making his work look natural that he’d run into objections from people when he tried to continue working on it (e.g. don’t you dare sully that pristine wilderness that you just built for us…)


Importantly, this reinforces our key idea!

  • Seeing some landscapes as natural and others as artificial is a serious problem

  • Let go of the natural vs artifical distinction and instead celebrate the wild in all its forms!
